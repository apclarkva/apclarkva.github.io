Research focus

I build computational models that connect molecular networks with cardiomyocyte function. My group uses gene regulatory network inference, logic-based modeling, and electrophysiology to understand how cardiac identity is established and how arrhythmias emerge.

Selected publications About me

Current themes

  • Cardiac development: Segment-specific gene regulatory subnetworks and chamber identity.
  • Electrophysiology: Mechanistic models of iPSC-derived cardiomyocytes and arrhythmia risk.
  • Systems tools: Logic-based network modeling for experimental collaborators.

Research directions

Gene regulatory networks in cardiac development

My postdoctoral work in the Saucerman lab focuses on how transcription factors like MEF2C and TBX5 orchestrate gene regulatory networks that drive heart tube morphogenesis and chamber specification.

Arrhythmia mechanisms & cardiotoxicity

I develop hybrid experimental-computational pipelines to interpret voltage-clamp and action-potential recordings, helping identify ionic mechanisms underlying drug-induced arrhythmias.

Translational modeling platforms

I build modeling toolkits (including Netflux workflows) that allow biologists and clinicians to test hypotheses about cardiac signaling pathways and perturbations.

Methods & approaches

My work combines experimental data with mechanistic models, emphasizing interpretability and translation. I regularly use high-throughput voltage clamp, single-cell electrophysiology, RNA-seq datasets, and dynamical systems modeling.

I am especially interested in collaborative research that integrates wet-lab experimentation with predictive modeling to inform therapeutic strategies.

Open to collaboration

I welcome collaborations that explore cardiac development, arrhythmia mechanisms, or data-driven network inference. Please reach out if you are interested in joint projects, workshops, or seminar invitations.
